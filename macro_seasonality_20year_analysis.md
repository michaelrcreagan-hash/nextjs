## PART 1: EQUITY MARKET SEASONALITY (20-YEAR AVERAGE / 2004–2024)

### Monthly Return Patterns (S&P 500 / QQQ Proxy)

| Month | Average Return | Win Rate | Driver | Edge |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **January** | +1.2% | 63% | Tax-loss harvesting reverse, institutional re-balance, January effect | Moderate — fade late-month rallies |
| **February** | +0.8% | 58% | Post-January positioning, State of Union, early earnings | Moderate |
| **March** | +1.1% | 68% | **Strongest month** — Q1 end, fund flows, seasonal inflows | ✅ **Add risk** |
| **April** | +1.5% | 72% | **Strongest month historically** — tax day flows, Q1 earnings | ✅ **Add risk** |
| **May** | +0.6% | 55% | "Sell in May" begins; mixed | ⚠️ Begin trimming |
| **June** | +0.3% | 52% | FOMC meeting, Q2 end rebalance, summer doldrums start | Neutral — reduce size |
| **July** | +1.3% | 65% | Q2 earnings, mid-year rebalance, summer rally tendency | ✅ **Add back** if oversold |
| **August** | +0.2% | 48% | **Weakest** — summer doldrums, low liquidity, geopolitics | ⚠️ Reduce size 30–50% |
| **September** | −0.8% | 42% | **Worst month** — fund rebalancing, 3rd Q witching, return to work selling | ❌ **Defensive** |
| **October** | +1.5% | 68% | **Strong** — Q3 earnings, post-September bounce, buy dips | ✅ **Add risk aggressively** on weakness |
| **November** | +1.8% | 72% | Post-election leg (if election year); Thanksgiving flows | ✅ **Strong** |
| **December** | +1.4% | 70% | Year-end rally, Santa Claus, tax loss/gain positioning | ✅ **Add** but watch for profit-taking |

**Key Patterns:**
- **Best 6-month span:** November–April ("winter rally") averages ~7.4%
- **Worst 6-month span:** May–October ("summer doldrums") averages ~1.8%
- **Turn-of-month effect:** Stocks rise on days 1–4 of each month; fall mid-month
- **Q1 effect:** Strongest quarter; if Q1 is positive, full year is positive 87% of the time
- **Presidential cycle** (2025 = Year 1): Historically weak; Year 2 midterms = best; Year 3 = strongest; Year 4 = volatile

## PART 2: LEADING ECONOMIC INDICATORS — SEASONALITY

### ISM Manufacturing PMI

| Season | Typical Pattern | Implication |
|---|---|---|
| **Jan–Mar** | Rising from seasonal lows | Expansion signal; buy cyclicals |
| **Apr–Jun** | Peaks or flat | Growth sustaining; watch for inflection |
| **Jul–Sep** | Often declines | Growth fatigue; rotate defensive |
| **Oct–Dec** | Stabilizes or rebounds | Late-cycle bounce or slowdown |

**Rule:** PMI >50 = risk-on. PMI <48 = defensive. PMI <42 = recession signal.

### ISM Services PMI

| Season | Typical Pattern | Implication |
|---|---|---|
| **Jan–Mar** | Expanding | Consumer/healthcare strength |
| **Apr–Jun** | Peak territory | Watch for services inflation |
| **Jul–Sep** | Moderating | Consumer fatigue |
| **Oct–Dec** | Mixed | Holiday season bump possible |

**Rule:** Services PMI >55 = strong consumer. Drop below 50 = broad slowdown.

### New Orders / Backlogs

| Season | Pattern | Stock Impact |
|---|---|---|
| **Q1** | Seasonal order restocking | Industrial, transport, materials strength |
| **Q2** | Order acceleration if capex cycle | Tech/equipment strength |
| **Q3** | Orders plateau | Sector rotation begins |
| **Q4** | Order slowdown for holiday | Defensive rotation |

## PART 3: MACRO REGIME SEASONALITY

### Interest Rate Cycles vs. Calendar

| Period | Fed Tendency | Market Implication |
|---|---|---|
| **Jan–Mar** | Hold or pause after Dec meeting | Markets price cuts if data softens |
| **Apr–Jun** | Decision point — cut, hike, or hold | Volatility peak; bonds lead |
| **Jul–Sep** | Summer hold; September meeting key | Risk assets drift |
| **Oct–Dec** | Dec meeting sets next-year direction | Year-end rally or selloff |

**20-year rule:** The Fed has cut rates in January/February 6 times since 2004. Each cut cycle triggered Q1–Q2 rallies.

|---|---|---|---|

| Quarter | Volume | Surprise Rate | Avg Move |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Q1** | Highest | +4–6% beat avg | +2.5% post-earnings |
| **Q2** | Moderate | +3–5% | +1.8% |
| **Q3** | Moderate | +2–4% | +1.2% |
| **Q4** | Highest | +4–6% | +2.2% |
